Executive Summary

Riverton's parking system hasn't been updated in over a decade. Permits still come in on paper and take five days to process. Enforcement officers carry handheld devices that only sync every six to eight hours, so they regularly ticket cars that actually have valid permits. The city collects only 58% of the fines it issues, and 311 parking complaints have been growing 8% every year.

We pulled 17 real datasets from 11 sources, mostly NYC city departments like Finance, Transportation, and 311, along with data from the MTA, the Census Bureau, and Open-Meteo for weather. We used New York City because it has the same parking problems as Riverton and makes all its data public. We cleaned and processed everything with Python and built an interactive dashboard using Next.js and Recharts, deployed on Vercel so anyone with the link can explore it.

What the data showed us is that there is no single system connecting permits, enforcement, finance, and citizen services. That one gap causes every other problem. Our solution is a cloud-based Parking Management System with four parts: an online citizen portal, a real-time mobile app for officers, an automated revenue tracking engine, and analytics dashboards for leadership. To test whether this would actually work, we generated two synthetic datasets of 5,000 records each and compared them side by side. The results: collection rate goes from 57.7% to 86.9%, wrongful citations drop from 15.7% to 2%, and permit processing shrinks from 8 days to about an hour.

Problem Context & Stakeholders

Current state of Riverton's parking operations and who is affected

Background

Riverton is a growing mid-sized city with a university, a busy downtown, and older neighborhoods that were never designed for this much traffic. The parking system running the whole operation was built years ago for a much smaller population. Staff call it "held together with tape," and honestly, looking at how it works, that's not an exaggeration.

The biggest issue is that nothing is connected. The Permit Office runs a legacy app on old servers. Some departments still take paper forms. Others use an online form that dumps into a spreadsheet. Enforcement officers walk around with handheld devices that only get updated through a batch upload a few times a day, meaning there's a 6-8 hour window where their data is stale. Finance tracks revenue on separate spreadsheets. And when a citizen calls 311 to complain, the person answering the phone has no way to check permit or enforcement records. Every department is its own island.

Data Cleaning

We cleaned all 17 datasets using pandas: removed duplicates, dropped records missing key fields (about 2-3% of records), standardized all dates to YYYY-MM-DD, filtered 311 data to parking-related complaints only, and converted string columns to proper numeric types for aggregation.

Executive Overview
Executive Overview: KPI cards, borough distribution, monthly trends, payment status

Brooklyn leads with 5,754 citations, nearly 1.5x more than Manhattan. These two boroughs together account for over half of all enforcement while Queens, Bronx, and Staten Island are under-served. The city issued 20,000 citations and received 20,000 complaints, meaning enforcement activity isn't reducing citizen frustration. The monthly trend shows two seasonal spikes (June and October) but near-zero activity in winter. 65% of citations are paid, but 34% carry late penalties, indicating people are paying late because there's no automated reminder system. The 107% collection rate is misleading as it's inflated by $182K in penalty fees, not efficient on-time collection.

Enforcement Dashboard
Enforcement: Hourly patterns, precinct breakdown with wrongful citations, borough distribution

Peak enforcement happens at 1pm with 1,017 citations. By 9pm it drops to nearly zero, meaning 75% of the day has little to no enforcement. Yet overnight violations like blocked driveways are among the top citizen complaints. Every precinct shows wrongful citations (the red bars), tickets given to cars with valid permits because the officer's device hadn't synced. The top 3 precincts handle nearly half of all citations while others are under-staffed. Brooklyn leads at nearly 6,000 citations, confirming enforcement is geographically stuck in familiar patterns rather than distributed based on actual demand.

Revenue & Finance
Revenue: Monthly collected vs outstanding, revenue by violation type, penalties summary

The city issued $1,265K in fines but collected $1,356K because $182K in late penalties inflated the total. Monthly revenue swings wildly with April at $120K and August near zero, following the same seasonal enforcement patterns. Photo School Zone Speed Violations generate roughly $280K in revenue, nearly double any other violation type, showing that automated enforcement is more consistent and profitable than manual officer patrols. The $12K outstanding balance may look small, but in Riverton's full-scale system this gap accumulates every month with no automated follow-up.

Citizen Experience
Citizens: 311 complaints by type, monthly trend, borough distribution

Only two complaint types dominate: Illegal Parking (~13,000) and Blocked Driveway (~7,000). These are residents calling to say someone is parked where they shouldn't be and nobody is doing anything about it. The trend is flat most of the year then explodes in October-November to nearly 20,000, a seasonal surge the current system can't predict. Brooklyn and Queens lead complaints at roughly 7,500 each, but Brooklyn also leads in citations. The borough getting the most enforcement is also generating the most complaints, proving that more tickets don't solve the underlying problem.

External Factors
External: Weather vs citations, events, transit ridership, population

The weather scatter shows citations clustered near zero regardless of temperature, demonstrating how inconsistent enforcement is under the legacy system. Sport Youth events dominate at roughly 13,000 permits, each removing parking spots and creating demand spikes the city can't anticipate. Manhattan has 430K+ subway riders while Staten Island has almost none, yet Staten Island gets the least enforcement despite higher parking demand from car-dependent residents. The current system responds to none of these external factors, using the same fixed schedule year-round.

Ethical & Social Considerations

Privacy, bias, inclusion, and transparency

Data Privacy

Permit applications have names, addresses, and plate numbers. Citation records are tied to individuals. The system needs encryption, role-based access so not every employee can see everything, and audit logs. State privacy regulations apply.

Enforcement Bias

Our data already shows Manhattan and Brooklyn get 60% of citations. If the dashboard drives deployment decisions, there's a risk of reinforcing existing concentration. The system should track equity metrics to distribute enforcement based on actual need, not historical patterns.

Digital Divide

Putting permits online is great for people with internet access. But lower-income residents, elderly people, and non-English speakers might get left behind. We recommend in-person kiosks at city offices and libraries, plus multilingual support.

Transparency

People should be able to see why they got a ticket, where their permit application stands, and how a dispute is being handled. The current paper system is a black box. The new system should be the opposite: clear status tracking at every step.

Lessons Learned & Team Reflection

What we learned, what we'd change, and how the team worked together

Sowjanya Chowdary Kavuri
Data Collection & Deployment

The hardest part for me was figuring out how to pull data from four completely different APIs. NYC Open Data uses Socrata, the Census Bureau has its own API format, Open-Meteo uses REST with different parameters, and NY State's portal looks similar to NYC's but has different quirks. I spent a lot of time just reading API documentation and handling errors when queries timed out or returned unexpected formats. Deploying on Vercel was actually the easy part once the code was on GitHub, but debugging build failures when a CSV file was too large or a path was wrong taught me a lot about how Next.js handles server-side data.

Sri Sharana Lingala
Data Cleaning & Modeling

I didn't expect data cleaning to take as long as it did. In class, cleaning sounds straightforward: remove duplicates, fix dates, drop nulls. In practice, every dataset had its own problems. The parking violations file used county codes like "MN" and "BK" instead of full borough names. The 311 file had complaint types that looked parking-related but weren't. Date formats varied between MM/DD/YYYY and ISO timestamps across files. Generating the synthetic datasets for the comparison was actually the most satisfying part because I could control every variable and see the exact impact of each rule change.

Sharanya Pakker
Dashboard Visualization

Building charts in Recharts instead of Power BI was a steep learning curve at first. In Power BI you drag a field onto a chart and it works. In Recharts, you write JSX components, handle responsive containers, style tooltips manually, and wire up every filter to state variables. But once I got comfortable, the control was worth it. I could make filters that recalculate KPI cards, not just highlight bars. The moment that felt most rewarding was when the borough filter on the Executive page made all four KPI cards change their numbers live.

Amruth Raju Melike
Analysis & Insights

My role was to connect the data to the story. We had thousands of data points, but a chart without interpretation is just a picture. The hardest part was being honest about what the data actually shows versus what we wanted it to show. The insight I'm most proud of is the connection between the Citizens page and the Enforcement page: the boroughs with the most enforcement also have the most complaints. That tells you the problem isn't that officers aren't working hard enough. It's the system they're working with.

Vani Meenakshi Kalva
Documentation & Formatting

I learned that documentation isn't just about writing things down after the work is done. It's about keeping everyone aligned while the work is happening. The responsibility matrix we set up in Week 2 was something I initially thought was just a formality, but it became the most important coordination tool we had. The most challenging part was the project charter because it required pulling together scope, resources, milestones, and constraints from every team member's area into one coherent document.

Team Takeaway

We underestimated how much time data work takes compared to building the actual dashboard. If we had the semester over again, we would spend the first two weeks setting up the data pipeline and deployment infrastructure before touching any charts. Our biggest revision was cutting the Overview and Permits pages late in development after an honest audit showed one was redundant and the other used the wrong dataset. That decision made the final dashboard stronger.
